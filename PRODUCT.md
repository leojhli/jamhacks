# Product

## Register

product

## Users

The Hub is for tired, overwhelmed high-school students, especially AP students, who open the app at night when they do not know where to start. They are juggling deadlines, tests, incomplete assignments, weak concepts, and limited energy. Their job is not to browse an academic dashboard; it is to decide the next best action and begin without spiraling.

## Product Purpose

The Hub turns deadlines, weak topics, available time, and energy level into the smartest plan for tonight. Success means a student can answer, "What should I do right now, why does it matter, what should I ignore, and what is the smallest first step?" The product should reduce pressure, prevent panic zones, and provide structure without completing work for the student.

## Brand Personality

Calm, decisive, humane, and premium. The voice is direct but non-shaming: it should feel like a sharp academic co-pilot helping the student regain control at 8 PM. The product should feel visually creative and memorable for a hackathon pitch, but never cute, childish, cluttered, or generic.

## Anti-references

Do not make The Hub look like a broad LMS, Google Classroom clone, generic admin dashboard, broad AI chatbot, homework solver, essay writer, mascot-driven school app, or cluttered analytics platform. Avoid cartoon visuals, bright school colors, dense feature sprawl, teacher-first dashboards, generic SaaS card grids, and UI that feels visibly AI-generated.

## Design Principles

1. Lead with one killer workflow: Plan My Night.
2. Give the student a clear next action before showing secondary information.
3. Explain every recommendation in plain, pressure-reducing language.
4. Tell students what not to do so the plan feels protective, not just additive.
5. Help students start the work without doing the work for them.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA basics. The app should include keyboard-friendly controls, high-contrast readable text, visible focus states, reduced-motion support, semantic HTML where possible, clear labels for buttons, inputs, cards, and navigation, and no critical information communicated by color alone.

Because The Hub is designed for tired and overwhelmed students using it at night, accessibility should support low-friction use: clear hierarchy, readable text, calm motion, and obvious actions. Premium visual design must never come at the expense of readability, contrast, or usability.
